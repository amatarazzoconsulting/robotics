## Volume I: Executive Summary and Strategic Justification

### Section 1: Executive Summary

The Quantum Materials Research Laboratory Complete Ecosystem (QMRL-1) is a proposed fully autonomous, artificial intelligence-driven research, development, fabrication, and deployment facility designed to revolutionize materials science, robotics, remote sensing, communications, and semiconductor manufacturing. The QMRL-1 ecosystem comprises nine integrated technical divisions spanning from atomic-scale material synthesis to macroscale environmental manipulation. The facility operates without daily human presence, with AI systems designing experiments, synthesizing materials, fabricating components, assembling complete instruments, deploying robotic systems, and learning from the results to continuously improve all processes.

The QMRL-1 addresses critical national needs identified in multiple federal strategic documents, including the DOE's Energy Storage Grand Challenge, the DOD's National Defense Strategy, the CHIPS and Science Act, and the NSF's 10 Big Ideas. The facility will accelerate the discovery of advanced materials for energy storage, electronics, and optics; fabricate next-generation sensors and communication systems; and deploy autonomous robots for infrastructure repair, environmental remediation, and disaster response. The total estimated cost of $1.45 billion over six years includes site acquisition, construction, equipment, personnel, and a five-year operational reserve. The facility is projected to generate an internal rate of return of 22% through technology licensing, direct product sales, and federal cost savings.

### Section 2: Strategic National Importance

The United States faces a critical gap in its ability to rapidly translate fundamental materials discoveries into operational technologies. The typical timeline from laboratory discovery to commercial deployment exceeds fifteen years, during which foreign competitors often capture the intellectual property and manufacturing base. The QMRL-1 compresses this timeline to under eighteen months by integrating discovery, fabrication, and deployment under a unified AI control system. This capability directly supports the CHIPS and Science Act's goal of restoring domestic semiconductor manufacturing, the DOE's objective of achieving grid-scale energy storage, and the DOD's need for autonomous systems and advanced sensors.

The facility's autonomous operation also addresses workforce challenges. The United States currently produces insufficient numbers of PhD scientists and engineers to staff traditional research laboratories at the scale required for national competitiveness. The QMRL-1 amplifies the productivity of each human researcher by a factor of approximately one hundred, allowing a small team of federal scientists to oversee a research program equivalent to ten thousand traditional laboratory personnel. This leverage multiplier enables the United States to compete effectively with nations that have larger scientific workforces.

### Section 3: Core Principles and Design Philosophy

The QMRL-1 ecosystem operates on five core principles that guide all design, construction, and operational decisions:

**Principle 1: Full Autonomy.** The facility is designed to operate continuously for months without any human presence. All routine operations—including experiment design, sample synthesis, characterization, data analysis, hypothesis generation, fabrication, assembly, testing, deployment, and maintenance—are performed by AI-controlled robotic systems. Humans enter only for strategic oversight, major repairs, or external review.

**Principle 2: Closed-Loop Learning.** Every action performed by the facility generates data that is used to improve future actions. The AI maintains unified knowledge graphs spanning all technical divisions, enabling transfer learning across domains. For example, a discovery in graphene synthesis in Division 1 is immediately available to optimize battery electrodes in Division 6 and radar antennas in Division 3.

**Principle 3: Material Closure.** The facility recycles over ninety percent of its material inputs. Worn-out components, failed experimental samples, and end-of-life devices are processed in a reclamation unit that separates constituent elements for reuse. The facility requires only bulk elemental feedstocks (silicon, carbon, copper, aluminum, iron, rare earths) and energy to operate indefinitely.

**Principle 4: Multi-Resolution Integration.** The facility operates seamlessly from the atomic scale (angstroms) to the macroscale (kilometers). Materials are characterized by electron microscopy. Components are assembled into robots. Robots are deployed across landscapes mapped by satellite. The AI maintains digital twins at all scales.

**Principle 5: Exportable Knowledge.** All discoveries and designs produced by the QMRL-1 are encoded in machine-readable formats suitable for direct ingestion by other AI systems. The facility automatically generates patent applications, scientific manuscripts, and technical reports for human review, ensuring that knowledge is disseminated to the broader scientific community.


## Volume II: Technical Facility Descriptions

### Section 4: Division 1 – Quantum Materials Synthesis Wing (Original QMRL-1 Core)

#### 4.1 Overview and Capabilities

The Quantum Materials Synthesis Wing is the foundational research engine of the QMRL-1 ecosystem. This division discovers and synthesizes novel materials with tailored electronic, optical, thermal, and mechanical properties. The wing operates twenty-four hours per day, seven days per week, producing thousands of unique material samples daily. Each sample is automatically characterized by a suite of instruments, and the resulting data is ingested into the AI's knowledge graph to guide subsequent experiments.

The wing contains eleven independent environmental chambers for controlled-atmosphere synthesis, four chemical vapor deposition systems for graphene and carbon nanotube growth, twelve parallel organic synthesis reactors for small-molecule and polymer preparation, eight quantum dot synthesis stations, and four high-throughput combinatorial sputtering systems for thin-film materials discovery. All synthesis equipment is connected to a central robotic sample handling system that transfers materials between synthesis stations, characterization instruments, and storage without human intervention.

#### 4.2 Graphene and Carbon Nanotube Synthesis

The wing operates four chemical vapor deposition (CVD) systems for the production of high-quality graphene and vertically aligned carbon nanotube arrays. Each CVD system contains a three-zone resistive heater capable of achieving temperatures up to 1100°C with ±1°C uniformity across a 200-millimeter substrate. Gas flow is controlled by eight mass flow controllers delivering methane, hydrogen, argon, nitrogen, oxygen, ammonia, diborane, and phosphine, enabling in-situ doping of graphene with nitrogen (n-type) or boron (p-type). The systems are equipped with real-time Raman spectroscopy for monitoring film quality during growth, allowing the AI to adjust temperature, pressure, and gas composition to optimize crystallinity and layer number. Typical growth runs produce monolayer graphene with domain sizes exceeding 100 micrometers and electron mobilities above 10,000 cm²/V·s. For carbon nanotube synthesis, the systems use plasma-enhanced CVD with an RF power supply operating at 13.56 MHz and 500 watts, producing vertically aligned forests with tube diameters of 1-10 nanometers and lengths up to 100 micrometers. The AI optimizes catalyst composition (iron, nickel, cobalt, or alloys) and pretreatment conditions to control diameter distribution and chirality.

#### 4.3 Organic Semiconductor and OLED Material Synthesis

The organic synthesis laboratory contains twelve parallel automated reactors operating under inert atmosphere (oxygen and water below 0.1 parts per million). Each reactor is equipped with a jacketed vessel for temperature control from -78°C to 250°C, overhead stirring, reflux condenser, and automated injection ports for reagents. The synthesis robots follow protocols generated by the AI to produce libraries of conjugated polymers, small-molecule emitters, host materials, and charge transport layers. Typical syntheses involve palladium-catalyzed cross-coupling reactions (Suzuki, Stille, Buchwald-Hartwig), with the AI optimizing catalyst loading, ligand choice, temperature, and reaction time to maximize yield and purity. After synthesis, products are purified by automated column chromatography (using robotic fraction collection) followed by temperature-gradient sublimation for final purification to >99.99% purity. The purified materials are characterized by nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy, high-resolution mass spectrometry, and high-performance liquid chromatography before being transferred to Division 4 for device fabrication. The AI maintains a database of over 50,000 synthesized compounds with complete synthetic provenance and characterization data.

#### 4.4 Quantum Dot and Nanoparticle Synthesis

The quantum dot synthesis area contains eight automated reactors for the production of colloidal quantum dots of II-VI (CdSe, CdS, CdTe), III-V (InP, InAs), IV-VI (PbS, PbSe), and perovskite (CsPbBr₃, CsPbI₃) compositions. Each reactor operates under inert atmosphere at temperatures from 100°C to 350°C, with automated injection of precursors (organometallic compounds, chalcogenides, halides) using syringe pumps. The AI controls reaction time, temperature profile, and precursor ratios to tune quantum dot diameter from 2 to 20 nanometers with ±0.2 nanometer uniformity, as verified by in-situ photoluminescence spectroscopy. After synthesis, quantum dots are purified by multiple cycles of centrifugation and redispersion, followed by ligand exchange to functionalize the surface for specific solvents or matrix materials. The automated ligand exchange station can exchange native oleic acid ligands for thiols, amines, carboxylic acids, or inorganic ligands (such as S²⁻ or Cl⁻) depending on the intended application. The purified quantum dots are characterized by transmission electron microscopy for size distribution, X-ray diffraction for crystal structure, and fluorescence spectroscopy for quantum yield (typically 50-95% depending on composition and structure). The facility produces gram-scale quantities of quantum dots daily, sufficient for thousands of test devices.

#### 4.5 High-Throughput Combinatorial Sputtering

For thin-film materials discovery, the wing operates four combinatorial sputtering systems, each containing eight independent sputtering guns arranged in a confocal geometry. The systems can deposit up to four materials simultaneously, with computer-controlled shutters enabling composition gradients across a 100-millimeter substrate. The AI designs libraries of thousands of compositions spanning phase space, such as (La,Sr)(Co,Fe)O₃ perovskites for solid oxide fuel cells or (Ge,Sb,Te) chalcogenides for phase-change memory. After deposition, the libraries are automatically characterized by X-ray fluorescence for composition mapping, X-ray diffraction for phase identification, four-point probe for sheet resistance, and spectroscopic ellipsometry for optical constants. The AI correlates composition with properties using machine learning models, identifying optimal compositions for further study. The combinatorial sputtering systems operate continuously, producing and characterizing approximately one hundred unique compositions per day per system, a rate that would require decades of traditional sequential experimentation.

### Section 5: Division 2 – Autonomous Tool & Robot Fabrication Facility (ATRFF-1)

#### 5.1 Overview and Capabilities

The Autonomous Tool & Robot Fabrication Facility receives materials from Division 1 and transforms them into functional robots, tools, sensors, actuators, and electronic systems. The division contains additive manufacturing, subtractive manufacturing, hybrid assembly, actuator fabrication, electronics fabrication, and quality assurance bays. All equipment is automated and coordinated by a central AI that translates high-level task requirements into complete manufacturing plans.

The facility produces robots ranging from millimeter-scale swimming robots for environmental monitoring to human-scale construction robots weighing 200 kilograms. It also fabricates end-effectors, grippers, specialized tools, and replacement parts for its own equipment. The division operates on a just-in-time inventory model, maintaining only minimal buffers of bulk materials, with most components fabricated on demand.

#### 5.2 Multi-Material Additive Manufacturing Core

The additive manufacturing core contains thirty-two parallel multi-material 3D printers, each equipped with sixteen independently controlled extrusion nozzles. The printers process a wide range of materials synthesized in Division 1, including graphene-reinforced thermoplastics with electrical conductivity up to 100 S/cm, carbon nanotube-filled elastomers for stretchable electronics, piezoresistive filaments for integrated force sensing, thermally conductive polymers for heat dissipation, and biodegradable scaffolds for tissue engineering applications. The printers achieve layer resolutions down to 5 microns and can transition between materials in under 200 milliseconds, enabling the fabrication of monolithic structures with embedded electrical, thermal, and mechanical functionality.

Each printer is equipped with a laser profilometer that performs in-situ layer inspection, detecting delamination, porosity, or dimensional errors at the time of printing. The AI adjusts process parameters (nozzle temperature, print speed, layer height, bed temperature) in real-time based on this feedback, maintaining dimensional accuracy to ±25 microns over 200-millimeter builds. The printers are enclosed in a temperature-controlled environment maintained at 22°C ±0.5°C with less than 5% relative humidity, eliminating thermal and moisture variables. The additive manufacturing core produces approximately 500 kilograms of finished components per day, ranging from microscopic grippers to structural frames.

#### 5.3 Precision Subtractive Manufacturing Bay

For applications requiring surface finishes below 1 micron or tolerances below 10 microns, the division operates twelve five-axis CNC machining centers with spindle speeds up to 50,000 revolutions per minute. The machining centers are capable of processing high-strength alloys (steel, titanium, Inconel), ceramics (alumina, zirconia, silicon carbide), and diamond-like carbon coatings. Each center is equipped with an automated tool changer containing over 500 unique cutting tools, including diamond-coated end mills for composite materials, polycrystalline diamond inserts for abrasive materials, and micro-tools with diameters down to 50 microns for fine features.

Workpieces are transferred between additive and subtractive stations via an overhead gantry system with positional accuracy of ±5 microns. The gantry system also transports components to the hybrid assembly station, where robotic arms equipped with force-torque sensors assemble multi-part mechanisms including gearboxes, linkages, and joint actuators. The assembly station incorporates machine vision systems with sub-micron resolution to guide alignment of precision components. All assembled mechanisms undergo functional testing on automated test stands that measure torque output, positional accuracy, backlash, and thermal performance.

The subtractive bay also contains an electrical discharge machining cell for creating features in materials that cannot be machined conventionally, such as tungsten carbide cutting edges or diamond heat spreaders. The wire EDM system achieves feature sizes down to 10 microns with surface finishes below 0.5 microns Ra. A laser micromachining station provides ablation capabilities with feature sizes down to 1 micron, enabling the creation of fluidic channels for pneumatic actuators and micro-optical elements for vision systems.

#### 5.4 In-House Actuator and Electronics Fabrication

The division does not rely on commercial actuators, sensors, or electronics. Instead, it fabricates these components entirely in-house using materials from Division 1. The actuator fabrication line produces custom electric motors using stator windings formed from graphene-silver composite conductors, which exhibit 60% higher conductivity than copper while weighing 70% less. Rotors are manufactured from neodymium-iron-boron magnets synthesized on-site using a robotic powder metallurgy system, achieving energy products exceeding 50 mega-gauss-oersteds. For linear actuation, the facility produces dielectric elastomer actuators from graphene-doped silicone films deposited via automated spin-coating. These actuators achieve strain rates up to 300% and energy densities of 3.4 joules per gram.

The electronics fabrication line produces custom circuit boards using direct-write lithography of silver-graphene inks, followed by automated pick-and-place assembly of surface-mount components that are themselves fabricated in-house via micro-dispensing of semiconductor inks. The printed circuit boards are encapsulated in parylene-C coatings deposited via chemical vapor deposition. For energy storage, the facility integrates graphene-based supercapacitors and solid-state battery cells directly into robot chassis structures. The dedicated battery assembly line produces pouch cells using electrode materials from Division 1, with capacities ranging from 100 milliamp-hours for microscale robots to 10 kilowatt-hours for human-scale construction robots.

### Section 6: Division 3 – Physical Environment Manipulation & Deployment Facility (PEMD-1)

#### 6.1 Overview and Capabilities

The Physical Environment Manipulation & Deployment Facility is the action arm of the QMRL-1 ecosystem. It receives robots and tools from Division 2, deploys them into the external environment, and coordinates their activities to achieve physical manipulation goals including construction, repair, exploration, environmental remediation, and disaster response. The facility serves as both a logistics hub and a command center, managing fleets of up to 5,000 autonomous robots operating simultaneously across a radius of 100 kilometers from the facility.

The PEMD-1 contains automated deployment bays, resupply vehicle depots, a robotic maintenance depot, and a centralized command center where the AI monitors and coordinates all field operations. The facility is hardened against environmental extremes, including earthquakes (magnitude 8.0), floods (500-year flood plain), hurricanes (Category 5 winds), and electromagnetic pulse events.

#### 6.2 Multi-Robot Deployment Systems

The deployment systems include ten automated launch bays, each capable of dispensing different classes of robots. For aerial robots, the bays contain pneumatic launch tubes that accelerate drones to takeoff velocity in under 1 second, followed by magnetic induction charging pads that top up batteries immediately before launch. For ground robots, the bays feature automated ramp systems that guide robots from their storage racks onto external terrain, with integrated wheel-washing stations to prevent cross-contamination. For aquatic robots, the bays are connected to a water channel that flows directly to an on-site pond and beyond to natural waterways.

The facility maintains a fleet of autonomous resupply vehicles: 18 wheeled robots (4-wheel drive, 500-kilogram payload, 100-kilometer range) and 6 aerial drones (quadcopter, 50-kilogram payload, 50-kilometer range). These vehicles transport spare parts, energy packs, and bulk materials to deployed robots on a just-in-time schedule determined by the central AI. The AI predicts resource consumption based on task duration, energy usage history, environmental conditions, and robot health monitoring data.

The deployment facility also includes a robotic maintenance depot where returned robots are cleaned, inspected, repaired, and recalibrated before redeployment. The depot contains 12 maintenance bays equipped with articulated robotic arms that can disassemble, diagnose, and reassemble robots in under 30 minutes for most classes. A dedicated battery conditioning station cycles returned batteries through controlled charge-discharge profiles to extend their operational lifetime. The entire depot operates autonomously, with the AI scheduling maintenance based on usage hours, performance degradation, and predicted future task requirements.

#### 6.3 Environmental Sensing and Digital Twin Infrastructure

Before deploying robots to manipulate the environment, the PEMD-1 first builds and maintains a comprehensive digital twin of the operational area. This twin is constructed from data collected by a persistent sensor network including ground-based LiDAR arrays, aerial survey drones, and satellite imagery. The ground-based LiDAR network consists of 50 fixed stations, each containing a 360-degree scanning unit with 100-meter range and 1-centimeter accuracy. These stations are mounted on telescoping masts that raise to 10 meters for unobstructed views and can be autonomously relocated.

Aerial survey drones, operating in teams of four, perform daily high-resolution mapping of the entire operational area, collecting both RGB and multispectral imagery at 10-centimeter resolution. The AI fuses this data into a unified point cloud representation that is updated every 6 hours, with dynamic elements (moving vehicles, people, animals) segmented and tracked separately. For underground or indoor environments where GPS and LiDAR are unavailable, the facility deploys small explorer robots equipped with simultaneous localization and mapping algorithms that construct 3D maps using time-of-flight depth sensors and inertial measurement units.

The digital twin incorporates environmental data streams including weather forecasts (1-kilometer resolution, 1-hour updates), soil moisture measurements from distributed wireless sensors (1,000 sensors covering the operational area), and air quality readings from fixed monitoring stations. The entire twin is maintained at sub-meter accuracy, with centimeter accuracy in zones where physical manipulation is actively occurring. The AI uses this twin to simulate manipulation tasks before physical execution, predicting outcomes and identifying potential failure modes.

#### 6.4 Specialized Manipulation Robot Fleets

The PEMD-1 maintains specialized fleets of robots for different classes of physical manipulation. The construction fleet consists of 100 heavy-lift hexacopters (50-kilogram payload each) and 50 quadrupedal ground robots equipped with 6-degree-of-freedom manipulator arms. These robots work collaboratively: quadrupeds position reinforcing bars or formwork while hexacopters deliver concrete or prefabricated components. The construction robots use stereovision and tactile sensing to achieve sub-centimeter placement accuracy.

The repair fleet includes 100 climbing robots equipped with suction cups or magnetic feet, capable of ascending vertical surfaces. These robots carry specialized end effectors for crack injection, surface cleaning, and coating application. For cracks less than 1 millimeter wide, robots inject low-viscosity epoxy that penetrates by capillary action. For larger defects, they apply polymer-modified cementitious patches that are smoothed and cured using integrated heating elements.

The environmental remediation fleet includes 50 aquatic robots for cleaning waterways, 50 ground robots for soil treatment, and 20 aerial dispersants for atmospheric treatment. Aquatic robots are equipped with microfiltration systems (1-micron pore size) and activated carbon filters that absorb dissolved contaminants. Soil treatment robots inject amendments at controlled depths, monitored by embedded sensors measuring pH, conductivity, and contaminant concentrations in real-time.

### Section 7: Division 4 – Remote Viewing & Electro-Optical Sensing Division

#### 7.1 Overview and Capabilities

The Remote Viewing & Electro-Optical Sensing Division designs and fabricates complete passive and active electro-optical systems for Earth observation, astronomical imaging, surveillance, and scientific remote sensing. The division produces instruments including multispectral scanners, hyperspectral imagers, thermal cameras, LiDAR systems, and synthetic aperture radar (SAR) systems. All optical, mechanical, and electronic components are fabricated in-house using materials from Division 1.

The division contains precision optical fabrication equipment, detector fabrication lines, assembly and alignment robots, and comprehensive calibration facilities. The division supports both space-qualified instruments for orbital deployment and ruggedized systems for airborne or ground-based operation.

#### 7.2 Hyperspectral and Multispectral Imaging Systems

The division operates a comprehensive design and fabrication line for pushbroom and framing hyperspectral imagers covering the ultraviolet (200-400 nanometers), visible (400-700 nanometers), near-infrared (700-1000 nanometers), shortwave infrared (1000-2500 nanometers), and thermal infrared (8-14 micrometers) spectral bands. Each imager is built around a custom diffractive grating or prism spectrometer that disperses incoming light onto a focal plane array.

The division fabricates diffraction gratings using electron-beam lithography on graphene-coated substrates, achieving groove densities up to 3600 lines per millimeter with blaze angles optimized for specific wavelength ranges. The lithography system operates at 100 keV accelerating voltage, achieving linewidths below 10 nanometers across 200-millimeter substrates. After lithography, the gratings are coated with aluminum, gold, or multilayer dielectric stacks to maximize reflectivity in the target spectral bands.

The dispersive elements are mounted in temperature-stabilized housings machined from Invar (a nickel-iron alloy with thermal expansion coefficient below 1.2 parts per million per degree Celsius), ensuring spectral calibration stability across operating temperatures from -40°C to +60°C. The focal plane arrays are fabricated in Division 9 (Silicon Printing), using either charge-coupled device or complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor technologies with pixel pitches as small as 5 microns. For hyperspectral systems, the division produces detectors with 1024 spatial pixels and 256 spectral channels, achieving spectral resolution better than 5 nanometers.

The complete imager is assembled in a class 100 cleanroom using automated alignment robots that position the grating, slit, and focal plane array with sub-micron accuracy. Prior to deployment, each imager undergoes radiometric calibration against traceable standards, including integrating sphere sources for radiance response and monochromator-based spectral calibration. The division can produce approximately 50 hyperspectral imagers per year, ranging from shoebox-sized instruments for small satellites to 100-kilogram systems for crewed aircraft.

#### 7.3 LiDAR and Active Imaging Systems

The division also produces light detection and ranging (LiDAR) systems for high-resolution 3D mapping. The LiDAR systems are built around pulsed fiber lasers operating at 1550 nanometers (eye-safe wavelength) with pulse energies of 100 microjoules, pulse widths of 1 nanosecond, and repetition rates of 100 kilohertz. The division fabricates the fiber lasers in-house using rare-earth-doped fibers (erbium or ytterbium) drawn from preforms produced in Division 1.

The receive optics consist of 100-millimeter diameter telescopes that collect backscattered light onto single-photon avalanche diode arrays (256 x 256 pixels) also fabricated in Division 9. The system achieves range resolution of 10 centimeters at distances up to 5 kilometers in daylight conditions. The division produces both scanning LiDAR (using a rotating polygon mirror for wide-area coverage) and flash LiDAR (using an array of detectors for snapshot imaging). The LiDAR systems are used for autonomous navigation, terrain mapping, vegetation canopy analysis, and bathymetric surveys.

#### 7.4 Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) Systems

For all-weather, day-night imaging capability, the division produces compact synthetic aperture radar systems operating in X-band (9.6 gigahertz), Ku-band (13.6 gigahertz), and Ka-band (35 gigahertz). The radar transceivers are built around gallium nitride solid-state power amplifiers fabricated in Division 9, achieving output powers up to 100 watts with 50% efficiency. The antennas are active electronically scanned arrays (AESA) containing 1000 to 10,000 individually controllable radiating elements.

The division fabricates the antenna elements using standard printed circuit board techniques with silver-graphene conductors. Each element is connected to a transmit/receive module containing a low-noise amplifier, phase shifter, and attenuator. The AESA achieves beam steering over ±60 degrees in both azimuth and elevation, with beamwidths as narrow as 1 degree. The SAR systems achieve spatial resolution of 10 centimeters at ranges up to 20 kilometers from airborne platforms, and 1 meter resolution from satellite orbit. The division produces SAR systems weighing less than 10 kilograms, suitable for deployment on small satellites or drones.

### Section 8: Division 5 – Radio Telescope & Antenna Division

#### 8.1 Overview and Capabilities

The Radio Telescope & Antenna Division designs and fabricates radio frequency reflectors, phased arrays, and complete radio astronomy instruments for frequencies from 10 megahertz to 300 gigahertz. The division supports both steerable parabolic antennas (up to 12 meters diameter) and stationary phased arrays (up to 100 meters aperture). The division also produces feeds, receivers, backends, and signal processing systems for radio astronomy, radar, and communications applications.

The division contains precision machining facilities for reflector panels, robotic assembly stations for feed arrays, and anechoic chambers for antenna characterization. All components are fabricated in-house using materials from Division 1, including carbon fiber composites for lightweight reflector panels and graphene-based conductors for low-loss transmission lines.

#### 8.2 Parabolic Reflector Antenna Fabrication

The division fabricates parabolic reflector antennas with diameters from 1 to 12 meters. The reflectors are constructed from carbon fiber reinforced polymer skins bonded to aluminum honeycomb cores, achieving surface accuracies better than 10 microns root-mean-square. The division operates a five-axis robotic filament winding system for the carbon fiber layers, followed by autoclave curing and precision machining of the mounting interfaces.

After fabrication, each reflector is measured using a laser tracker and photogrammetry system to verify surface accuracy. The division also produces the antenna mounts, including alt-azimuth and equatorial designs, with drive systems capable of pointing accuracy of 0.001 degrees and slew rates up to 10 degrees per second. The complete 12-meter antenna system weighs less than 5,000 kilograms and can be transported in standard shipping containers for field deployment.

The division fabricates the feed systems for these antennas, including corrugated conical horns, scalar rings, and orthomode transducers. The feeds are machined from aluminum or copper using the subtractive manufacturing bay, then plated with silver or gold for low-loss performance. The division also produces cryogenic receivers that cool the low-noise amplifiers to 15 kelvin using closed-cycle cryocoolers, achieving noise temperatures below 10 kelvin at frequencies up to 100 gigahertz.

#### 8.3 Phased Array Antenna Fabrication

For applications requiring rapid beam steering without moving parts, the division fabricates active electronically scanned arrays (AESA) covering frequencies from 100 megahertz to 100 gigahertz. The arrays are constructed from multilayer printed circuit boards containing the radiating elements, phase shifters, attenuators, and distribution networks.

The division produces arrays ranging from 16-element demonstration systems to 100,000-element full-scale instruments. For low-frequency arrays (100-1000 megahertz), the elements are dipole or bowtie antennas printed on flexible substrates that can be rolled for transport. For high-frequency arrays (10-100 gigahertz), the elements are patch antennas fabricated using photolithography on gallium arsenide or silicon substrates.

Each array element is connected to a transmit/receive module containing a low-noise amplifier (noise figure below 1 decibel), phase shifter (6-bit resolution, 360-degree range), and attenuator (dynamic range 30 decibels). The modules are fabricated using surface-mount assembly robots and tested individually before integration. The division also produces the beamforming network that combines signals from all elements, using either analog (corporate feed) or digital (direct sampling) architectures.

#### 8.4 Digital Backend and Signal Processing

For radio astronomy observations, the division produces digital backends that digitize the received signals and perform correlation, filtering, and spectral analysis. The backends are built around field-programmable gate arrays (FPGAs) and graphics processing units (GPUs) fabricated in Division 9.

A typical backend for a 100-element array digitizes each polarization at 1 giga-sample per second with 8-bit resolution, producing 1.6 gigabytes per second of raw data. The FPGAs perform real-time channelization (using polyphase filter banks with up to 1 million frequency channels), correlation (computing all cross-correlations between element pairs), and detection. The processed data is stored on local solid-state drives and transmitted to the central AI for analysis via fiber optic links. The division produces backends for both connected-element interferometers (correlating signals between physically separate antennas) and phased arrays (coherently combining element signals).

### Section 9: Division 6 – Precision Lens Crafting Division

#### 9.1 Overview and Capabilities

The Precision Lens Crafting Division fabricates high-quality refractive, reflective, and diffractive optical elements from glass, crystalline materials, and novel transparent ceramics. The division produces lenses ranging from millimeters to meters in diameter, for applications including microscopy, lithography, astronomy, laser systems, and imaging. All lenses are fabricated in-house from raw optical materials synthesized or acquired by Division 1.

The division contains computer-controlled grinding and polishing machines, interferometric test stations, coating chambers, and assembly robots. The division achieves surface accuracies better than 1 nanometer root-mean-square and figure accuracies better than lambda/20 (where lambda = 633 nanometers) on most lenses.

#### 9.2 Lens Blank Preparation

Raw optical materials (fused silica, BK7, SF11, calcium fluoride, magnesium fluoride, sapphire, zinc selenide, and novel graphene-doped glasses) are received as cast ingots or synthesized via chemical vapor deposition. The division operates a precision slicing saw that cuts the ingots into lens blanks with thickness uniformity better than 0.1 millimeters. The blanks are then ground to approximate shape using a computer-controlled generating machine with diamond-impregnated grinding wheels.

The generating machine operates at spindle speeds up to 10,000 revolutions per minute, removing material at rates up to 10 cubic centimeters per minute while maintaining dimensional accuracy of ±10 microns. The division processes approximately 1,000 lens blanks per week, ranging in diameter from 2 millimeters to 500 millimeters.

#### 9.3 Precision Grinding and Polishing

Lens blanks progress to the precision grinding stations, where computer-controlled optical surfacing machines shape the surfaces to final curvature. These machines use smaller diamond grinding tools that follow the desired surface profile, achieving surface figure accuracies of ±1 micron after fine grinding. The division operates 20 grinding spindles, each equipped with in-situ profilometers that measure surface shape during processing and provide feedback for tool path correction.

After grinding, lenses move to the polishing stations for surface smoothing. The division operates 40 computer-controlled polishing machines using pitch or polyurethane laps with cerium oxide, aluminum oxide, or diamond slurries. Polishing times range from 15 minutes for small spherical lenses to 48 hours for large aspheric lenses. The division achieves surface roughness below 0.5 nanometers root-mean-square and figure accuracies below lambda/20 on standard lenses, and lambda/50 on precision optics.

For aspheric lenses, the division uses magnetorheological finishing (MRF) for final figure correction. MRF uses a magnetic field to control the viscosity of a polishing slurry, allowing localized material removal at the sub-micron scale. The MRF machine measures the lens surface with an interferometer, calculates the required material removal map, and executes the correction in an automated loop that converges to the target figure in typically 3-5 iterations.

#### 9.4 Optical Coating Deposition

After polishing, lenses receive anti-reflection, high-reflection, or bandpass coatings in the division's coating chambers. The division operates four electron-beam evaporation systems with ion-assisted deposition. Each chamber contains multiple crucibles for different coating materials (magnesium fluoride, silicon dioxide, titanium dioxide, tantalum pentoxide, gold, silver, aluminum). The chambers achieve base pressures below 1 x 10⁻⁶ Torr and can coat up to 20 lenses per batch.

The AI optimizes coating designs for specific wavelength ranges and angles of incidence using thin-film interference models. Typical anti-reflection coatings achieve reflectivity below 0.1% across the visible spectrum; high-reflection coatings achieve reflectivity above 99.99% at specific wavelengths. The division also produces notch filters, dichroic beamsplitters, and broadband polarizers. After coating, each lens is tested using spectrophotometry to verify spectral performance.

### Section 10: Division 7 – Electronic Lens & Metasurface Division

#### 10.1 Overview and Capabilities

The Electronic Lens & Metasurface Division fabricates flat optical elements that use sub-wavelength nanostructures to control light. These metalenses, diffractive optical elements, and flat optics are fabricated using semiconductor lithography techniques, enabling compact, lightweight optical systems that cannot be made with traditional curved lenses.

The division contains electron-beam lithography systems, nanoimprint lithography tools, reactive ion etching chambers, atomic layer deposition systems, and scanning electron microscopes for metrology. The division fabricates metalenses for wavelengths from the ultraviolet (250 nanometers) to the long-wave infrared (12 micrometers).

#### 10.2 Metalens Design and Simulation

The AI designs metalenses using electromagnetic simulation software based on finite-difference time-domain (FDTD) and rigorous coupled-wave analysis (RCWA). The design process begins with the target specifications: focal length, numerical aperture, operating wavelength, and desired aberrations correction. The AI searches the library of nanostructure shapes (cylindrical pillars, elliptical posts, rectangular blocks, holes) and materials (silicon, titanium dioxide, gallium nitride, amorphous silicon, germanium) to identify structures that provide the required phase profile.

Each metalens contains millions of nanostructures arranged in a sub-wavelength lattice with periods typically between 200 and 500 nanometers. The phase shift imparted by each structure is determined by its dimensions (diameter, height, ellipticity, orientation), with the AI generating a complete phase map that implements the desired lens function. The division maintains a database of over 1 million characterized nanostructures with measured phase and transmission values.

#### 10.3 Metalens Fabrication

The division fabricates metalenses using electron-beam lithography for master patterns, followed by nanoimprint lithography for replication. The electron-beam lithography system operates at 100 keV accelerating voltage, achieving linewidths of 10 nanometers across 200-millimeter wafers. The system writes the nanostructure pattern directly onto electron-beam resist, with write times ranging from 1 hour for small-diameter (1 millimeter) metalenses to 24 hours for full-wafer patterns.

After development, the pattern is transferred into the underlying material (silicon, titanium dioxide, etc.) using reactive ion etching. The division operates four etching chambers with inductively coupled plasma sources, using chemistries optimized for each material (fluorine-based for silicon, chlorine-based for gallium nitride). The etch process achieves aspect ratios of 10:1 with sidewall verticality of ±2 degrees.

For mass production, the division uses nanoimprint lithography to replicate metalenses from a single master. The master is used to stamp the pattern into a UV-curable resin on a glass or polymer substrate. The division operates two nanoimprint tools that can process 200-millimeter wafers in under 5 minutes each, enabling production of thousands of metalenses per day.

#### 10.4 Metrology and Testing

Each metalens is characterized using a dedicated optical test station that measures focusing efficiency, point spread function, modulation transfer function, and stray light rejection. The test station contains a tunable laser source (250-4000 nanometers), motorized stages for positioning, and a CCD camera for beam profiling. The AI compares measured performance to simulated performance and adjusts the fabrication parameters for subsequent batches to correct any deviations. The division achieves metalenses with focusing efficiencies exceeding 80% and diffraction-limited spot sizes.

### Section 11: Division 8 – Complete Communications Systems Division

#### 11.1 Overview and Capabilities

The Complete Communications Systems Division designs and fabricates integrated communication transceivers, networks, and systems for applications including terrestrial wireless, satellite communications, deep-space links, quantum communication, and optical interconnects. The division produces complete systems from antennas and amplifiers to modems and network protocols.

The division contains radio frequency, optical, and quantum communication fabrication and test facilities. All components are fabricated in-house using materials and devices from other divisions.

#### 11.2 RF Communications Systems

The division produces software-defined radios operating from 1 megahertz to 100 gigahertz. Each radio consists of a wideband receiver and transmitter built around direct digital synthesis and direct sampling architectures. The division fabricates the analog front-end components (low-noise amplifiers, power amplifiers, mixers, filters) using gallium nitride and gallium arsenide processes in Division 9.

The software-defined radio uses field-programmable gate arrays for real-time signal processing, implementing modulation schemes (QPSK, 16-QAM, 64-QAM, OFDM), error correction codes (LDPC, turbo, polar), and multiple access protocols (TDMA, FDMA, CDMA, OFDMA). The division produces radios ranging from chip-scale devices (1 cubic centimeter) for CubeSats to rack-mounted systems (50 kilograms) for ground stations.

The division also produces phased array antennas for beamforming and null steering, as described in Division 5. The combination of phased arrays and software-defined radios enables adaptive communications that automatically optimize link performance in response to changing channel conditions.

#### 11.3 Free-Space Optical Communications

For high-bandwidth applications, the division produces free-space optical communication terminals operating at wavelengths of 1550 nanometers (eye-safe) and 1064 nanometers (high power). Each terminal contains a fiber laser transmitter (up to 10 watts output), a fast-steering mirror for beam pointing (accuracy 1 microradian, bandwidth 2 kilohertz), a receiver based on single-photon counting modules, and an acquisition and tracking system using a separate camera.

The division achieves data rates up to 100 gigabits per second over terrestrial links up to 10 kilometers, and 10 gigabits per second over satellite-to-ground links through atmospheric turbulence. The terminals are used for high-bandwidth data relay, secure communications, and deep-space links.

#### 11.4 Quantum Communication Systems

The division produces quantum key distribution (QKD) systems for secure communications based on the principles of quantum mechanics. The QKD systems use single photons transmitted through fiber or free space, with polarization or phase encoding of quantum bits. The division fabricates single-photon sources using quantum dots or parametric down-conversion, and single-photon detectors using superconducting nanowires or avalanche photodiodes.

The division produces both fiber-based QKD (up to 100 kilometers range) and free-space QKD (up to 10 kilometers terrestrial, 500 kilometers satellite-to-ground). The systems generate secure key rates up to 1 megabit per second over short distances. The division also produces entanglement-based systems for quantum repeater and quantum network nodes.

### Section 12: Division 9 – Silicon Printing & Semiconductor Division

#### 12.1 Overview and Capabilities

The Silicon Printing & Semiconductor Division is the most advanced facility in the QMRL-1 ecosystem, responsible for fabricating all electronic components used throughout the other divisions. The division produces custom processors, memory, analog circuits, radio frequency chips, power electronics, and sensors. The division operates at technology nodes from 180 nanometers (for power and analog) to 3 nanometers (for highest-performance digital logic).

The division contains cleanrooms, lithography tools, deposition systems, etch chambers, metrology equipment, and test stations. All processes are automated and controlled by the central AI, with wafers transported between tools by robotic carriers.

#### 12.2 Semiconductor Wafer Fabrication

The division processes 300-millimeter silicon wafers through a sequence of hundreds of processing steps. The core of the fabrication line is the lithography bay, containing five deep ultraviolet (DUV) steppers (193 nanometer wavelength) and two extreme ultraviolet (EUV) scanners (13.5 nanometer wavelength). The DUV steppers achieve resolution of 38 nanometers and throughput of 200 wafers per hour. The EUV scanners achieve resolution of 13 nanometers and throughput of 100 wafers per hour, enabling production of 3-nanometer and 5-nanometer node devices.

The division also contains an electron-beam lithography system for small-volume or experimental devices (as described in Division 7). The e-beam system achieves resolution of 10 nanometers but with lower throughput, used for prototype circuits and metalens masters.

The division operates a full suite of deposition systems for thin films: atomic layer deposition (ALD) for high-k dielectrics (hafnium oxide, zirconium oxide, aluminum oxide), chemical vapor deposition (CVD) for silicon dioxide, silicon nitride, and polysilicon, physical vapor deposition (PVD) for metal layers (copper, aluminum, titanium, tungsten, cobalt), and metal-organic chemical vapor deposition (MOCVD) for III-V compound semiconductors (gallium nitride, gallium arsenide, indium phosphide).

The etch bays contain reactive ion etchers (RIE) for dielectric etching, inductively coupled plasma etchers (ICP-RIE) for high-aspect-ratio silicon etching, and ion beam etchers for metal removal. The division maintains separate wet processing bays for cleaning (RCA clean, piranha, HF dip) and metal lift-off.

#### 12.3 Processor and Memory Fabrication

The division produces custom processors optimized for the AI workloads of the QMRL-1. Each processor combines high-performance CPU cores (based on RISC-V architecture), massively parallel GPU cores (optimized for neural network inference and training), and specialized accelerators (for FFT, matrix multiplication, cryptographic operations). The division fabricates processors with up to 100 billion transistors on a single chip, operating at frequencies up to 4 gigahertz.

For memory, the division fabricates both volatile (SRAM, DRAM) and non-volatile (flash, phase-change, resistive RAM) memory. The division produces high-bandwidth memory stacks using through-silicon vias and micro-bump bonding, achieving memory bandwidths exceeding 1 terabyte per second.

The division also fabricates analog and mixed-signal circuits, including analog-to-digital converters (resolution up to 24 bits, sampling rates up to 10 giga-samples per second), digital-to-analog converters, phase-locked loops, and voltage regulators. For radio frequency applications, the division produces gallium nitride and gallium arsenide devices with cutoff frequencies exceeding 200 gigahertz.

#### 12.4 Silicon Photonics and Heterogeneous Integration

The division fabricates silicon photonic integrated circuits for optical communications, sensing, and computing. The silicon photonics process adds waveguide layers, germanium photodetectors, silicon modulators, and grating couplers to the standard CMOS process. The division produces transceivers with 32 channels operating at 100 gigabits per channel, achieving aggregate bandwidth of 3.2 terabits per second.

For advanced systems, the division performs heterogeneous integration, combining chips fabricated in different technologies (CMOS logic, analog, memory, photonics, III-V devices) into a single package. The division uses chiplet-based assembly with high-density interconnects (pitch as small as 10 microns) fabricated using the precision subtractive manufacturing bay. The division also performs wafer-to-wafer and die-to-wafer bonding using hybrid bonding, oxide-oxide bonding, and copper-thermocompression techniques.


## Volume III: Facility-Wide Systems

### Section 13: Central AI Control System

#### 13.1 Compute Cluster Specifications

The QMRL-1's central AI system is built around a massive compute cluster located in a purpose-built data center within the facility. The cluster contains 512 NVIDIA H100 GPUs (80 gigabytes HBM3 each, providing 3,584 teraFLOPS of FP8 compute and 896 teraFLOPS of FP64 compute), 256 AMD EPYC 9654 CPUs (192 cores each, 2.4 gigahertz, total 49,152 cores), and 512 terabytes of DDR5 ECC memory. Storage is provided by a 50-petabyte NVMe array (PCIe Gen5, 15 gigabytes per second read/write) for active data, and a 500-petabyte LTO-9 tape library with robotic retrieval for archival storage.

The cluster is interconnected by NVIDIA Quantum-2 InfiniBand providing 400 gigabit per second non-blocking bandwidth, with a two-level fat-tree topology that minimizes latency. The cluster consumes 10 megawatts of power and is liquid-cooled using direct-to-chip cooling, achieving a power usage effectiveness of 1.05.

#### 13.2 AI Software Architecture

The AI software stack comprises multiple layers. The lowest layer is the LOWL real-time kernel that controls all facility equipment, providing deterministic response with jitter below 1 microsecond. Above that, the NEBULA distributed object OS manages the knowledge graph, coordinates distributed tasks across the compute cluster, and provides fault tolerance.

The AI applications themselves include material discovery models (graph neural networks for property prediction, generative models for inverse design), experiment planning algorithms (Bayesian optimization, active learning, reinforcement learning), characterization analysis (convolutional neural networks for image segmentation, recurrent neural networks for time-series analysis), manufacturing control (computer vision for inspection, model predictive control for process optimization), and deployment coordination (swarm intelligence algorithms, digital twin simulation).

### Section 14: Power, Water, and Logistics

#### 14.1 Power Generation and Distribution

The QMRL-1 is designed to be energy self-sufficient. A solar farm covering 200 acres provides 20 megawatts of peak photovoltaic generation, with 50 megawatt-hours of lithium iron phosphate battery storage providing 4 hours of backup power. For continuous operation, the facility is connected to the grid with a dual-feed 20 megawatt connection from two separate substations. For extended outages, the facility contains four 5-megawatt natural gas generators with on-site fuel storage for 7 days of continuous operation at full load.

Power distribution throughout the facility is at 480 volts three-phase for major equipment, stepped down to 208 volts three-phase and 120 volts single-phase at point of use. Critical systems (AI compute cluster, environmental chambers, medical/life support) are served by uninterruptible power supplies providing 30 minutes of ride-through time.

#### 14.2 Water Supply and Treatment

The facility consumes approximately 500,000 gallons of water per day, primarily for cooling and for laboratory processes. Water is supplied from a dedicated well field on the property (six wells, 500 gallons per minute each) with a 50 million gallon reservoir for storage. The water treatment plant produces three grades: potable water for human use (meeting Safe Drinking Water Act standards), process water for rinsing and cooling (reverse osmosis, 10 megohm resistivity), and ultra-pure water for semiconductor fabrication (18.2 megohm, total organic carbon below 1 part per billion).

#### 14.3 Logistics and Material Flow

Bulk raw materials (silicon, carbon, copper, aluminum, iron, rare earth elements, etc.) are delivered by rail to a receiving depot at the facility boundary. Autonomous forklifts unload the rail cars and transfer materials to storage silos or to the material processing area. The material processing area performs initial refinement (crushing, grinding, separation, purification) before materials are distributed to the individual divisions.

Finished products (lenses, sensors, circuits, robots, etc.) are packaged by automated systems and stored in a warehouse with capacity for 10,000 pallets. Orders are fulfilled by autonomous trucks that load from the warehouse and deliver to shipping docks for rail or truck transport to customers.


## Volume IV: Project Implementation Plan

### Section 15: Site Selection and Acquisition (Months 1-6)

The QMRL-1 requires a site of at least 2,000 acres in a location with low seismic activity (Seismic Zone 0 or 1), no flood risk (500-year flood plain), adequate water supply, and access to rail and major highways. Candidate states identified include Texas, Arizona, New Mexico, Nevada, and Colorado. Site selection will be based on a weighted scoring system evaluating infrastructure availability, regulatory environment, workforce proximity, and cost. A single site will be selected by Month 3, with acquisition and permitting completed by Month 6.

### Section 16: Design and Engineering (Months 1-12)

Concurrent with site selection, the detailed engineering design will proceed. The design team includes architects, structural engineers, mechanical engineers, electrical engineers, and process engineers. The design will be completed in Building Information Modeling (BIM) format, with a complete digital twin of the facility produced before any construction begins. All equipment specifications will be finalized, and long-lead items will be ordered starting at Month 6.

### Section 17: Construction and Installation (Months 6-42)

Construction will proceed in four phases. Phase 1 (Months 6-18) includes site preparation, foundations, underground utilities, and the central AI tower. Phase 2 (Months 12-30) includes the research wings (Divisions 1, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9). Phase 3 (Months 24-36) includes the fabrication and deployment wings (Divisions 2 and 3). Phase 4 (Months 30-42) includes finishing, interior installation, and equipment commissioning.

### Section 18: Equipment Installation and Testing (Months 36-48)

As each building is completed, equipment will be moved in, installed, and connected to power, water, and data networks. The AI system will be installed and trained. Each piece of equipment will undergo acceptance testing, followed by integration testing across equipment groups, and finally end-to-end testing of complete workflows.

### Section 19: System Integration and Shakedown (Months 42-54)

The facility will operate initially with human supervision, performing test workflows that exercise all divisions and inter-division transfers. The AI will be trained on supervised data, then gradually given more autonomy. By Month 48, the facility will be capable of autonomous operation for 24-hour periods. By Month 54, the facility will be capable of autonomous operation for 30-day periods. Final acceptance will occur at Month 54.

### Section 20: Full Operational Capability (Month 54 onward)

At Month 54, the QMRL-1 will achieve Full Operational Capability, with the ability to operate indefinitely without human presence. A small team of 20 scientists, engineers, and technicians will provide oversight, strategic direction, and external stakeholder interaction. The facility will operate for an expected 30-year lifetime before major refurbishment.

### Section 21: Risk Management

Key technical risks include: (1) AI safety and control, mitigated by layered architecture with hardware interlocks and human review for high-consequence actions; (2) equipment reliability, mitigated by redundancy (N+1 or N+2 for critical systems), predictive maintenance, and on-site spares; (3) material supply disruptions, mitigated by strategic reserves, material recycling, and multiple suppliers; (4) cybersecurity threats, mitigated by air-gapped networks, encryption, intrusion detection, and regular penetration testing; (5) regulatory compliance, mitigated by dedicated compliance staff and integrated auditing systems.

The QMRL-1 will operate under all applicable federal, state, and local environmental regulations, including the Clean Air Act, Clean Water Act, Resource Conservation and Recovery Act, and Toxic Substances Control Act. ## Volume VII: Conclusion and Recommendation

### Section 30: Strategic Value to the United States

The QMRL-1 represents a strategic investment in American scientific and technological leadership. The facility will: (1) accelerate materials discovery by 100-fold relative to current methods, (2) re-establish domestic semiconductor manufacturing capability, (3) develop autonomous systems for infrastructure repair and disaster response, (4) advance energy storage technology to support grid-scale renewable energy, and (5) train the next generation of AI-accelerated scientists through remote research participation.

The facility's autonomous operation amplifies the productivity of a small federal workforce, achieving research output equivalent to 10,000 traditional laboratory personnel. This leverage multiplier enables the United States to compete effectively with nations that have larger scientific workforces, without requiring a proportional increase in federal research funding.

The total cost of $1.66 billion over 54 months is modest relative to comparable investments (e.g., the semiconductor CHIPS Act provides $52 billion for multiple facilities; the Human Genome Project cost $2.7 billion in current dollars). The projected 12.5% internal rate of return compares favorably to typical federal research investments, which are evaluated on the basis of societal benefit rather than financial return.

### Section 31: Recommendation

Based on the technical feasibility, strategic importance, and favorable economic analysis presented in this document, we recommend that the Department of Energy, Department of Defense, and National Science Foundation jointly fund the Quantum Materials Research Laboratory Complete Ecosystem at the requested level of $1.66 billion over 54 months. We further recommend that the facility be operated as a Federally Funded Research and Development Center (FFRDC), with management contracted to a qualified non-profit operator.
